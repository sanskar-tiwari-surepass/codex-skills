# Natural Content Writing

## Overview

Write like a person with a point of view. Prefer specifics, plain syntax, uneven rhythm, and earned emphasis over generic uplift, stock transitions, inflated significance, and chatbot filler.

## Workflow

1. Identify the job: audience, goal, format, and what the reader should think, feel, or do after reading.
2. Choose a voice anchor before drafting, such as "dry and direct", "warm but unsentimental", or "practical and sharp".
3. Lead with the real point. Do not open with broad scene-setting unless the brief clearly wants it.
4. Build paragraphs from concrete facts, examples, stakes, or observations. If a sentence could fit dozens of unrelated topics, rewrite it or cut it.
5. Run the anti-tell pass before returning the draft.

## Anti-Tell Pass

- Cut inflated framing such as "pivotal", "transformative", "underscores the importance", "serves as a testament", or "in today's evolving landscape" unless the brief explicitly calls for that register.
- Replace broad significance or legacy language with named consequences, examples, numbers, or actors.
- Remove vague attribution like "many believe", "widely regarded", or "critics and audiences alike" unless the source or speaker is named.
- Break formulaic contrasts such as "not just X, but Y" and "not X, but Y". State the point directly.
- Break rigid triads. If the draft keeps landing on three-item lists, rewrite some of them into one sharp phrase or two uneven items.
- Prefer plain verbs and basic sentence shapes when they are the clearest option. Do not contort prose to sound elevated.
- Keep structure proportional. Use lists only when the content is actually list-shaped.
- Avoid title-case headings, excessive boldface, emoji, and em-dash-heavy rhythm unless the target style clearly wants them.
- Strip chatbot residue addressed to the user, including collaborative framing, placeholder scaffolding, and "let me know if you'd like" endings.

## Editing Rules

- Keep the strongest noun in each sentence concrete when possible.
- Let repetition stand when it improves clarity; do not reach for decorative synonyms just to vary wording.
- Avoid summary conclusions that retreat into vague "challenges, opportunities, and future outlook" language.
- Do not force slang, faux informality, or quirks just to sound human. Natural is not the same as casual.
- When the brief allows, leave a little texture in the sentence flow. Uniform polish is often the tell.
